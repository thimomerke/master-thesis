\mychapter{3}{Techno-economic modeling of NETs}
In order to develop optimal portfolios of negative emission technologies, the four relevant approaches were systematically modeled from a technological and economic perspective. The goal of these techno-economic models was to calculate the country-level availability and cost of these NET's in order to construct a global marginal abatement cost curve (MACC). These MACC's serve as the answer to the first research question, and, more importantly, provide the basis for the optimization models used to generate optimal portfolios of negative emission technologies delivering the required carbon removals.

\section{Methodology}
Generally, a bottom-up process was used to construct techno-economic models of the four relevant NET's. For each NET, the entire process from feedstock input to the final output of the carbon credits was modeled. This included the capital investments required to construct exemplary projects or plants, as well as the fixed and variable costs of operating these plants, primarily materials, energy and labour. The models also included relevant leakage factors from production, transport and storage. In generic terms, the LCOC is calculated as
\begin{equation}
\text{LCOC} = w + f + c = w + \frac{\sum{PV(F_t)}}{\sum{CDR_t}} + \frac{SP}{\sum{CDR_t}}*CRF
\end{equation}
where $w$ is the variable cost per tonne of CDR, $PV(F)$ is the present value of the annual fixed costs for operation and maintenance, $SP$ is the system price (i.e., the capital investment) required for the project, and $CRF$ is the capital recovery factor, which, at an assumed discount rate of 7\% and project lifetime of 20 years, is set at 9.44\%. For the other LCOC-derived terms, such as the LCOCR, the costs of transportation and storage are added, co-revenues deducted, and the leakage factor applied to the sum of CDR.\vspace{2mm}

For the input data, country-level data on the availability and, if relevant, cost of the primary constraints, such as land and biomass, was gathered primarily from the FAOSTAT database \parencite{fao_faostat_2025, fao_faostat_2025-1, fao_faostat_2026}. Additionally, country-level data on the cost of labor, materials and other relevant inputs was collected. If such data was not available, global averages were calculated and adjusted for each country using purchasing power parity (PPP) data of the World Bank \parencite{world_bank_icp_2021}. The collected data was then fed into the techo-economic models in order to build a global marginal abatement cost curve (MACC) for each NET.

\mychapter{4}{Development of optimal global CDR portfolios}
To construct the ideal portfolios, two types of optimization approaches were considered. First, a simple static portfolio was created, which is based on a one-off removal of a given quantity of carbon dioxide on a given timeline, such as the removal of ten gigatonnes of CO$_2$ over five years. In the second step, this simple static approach was evolved into a dynamic portfolio approach, which considers a changing (increasing) annual requirement of CDR in order to reach certain targets on a certain timeline, such as the annual removal of 10 gigatonnes of CO$_2$ by the year 2050.\vspace{2mm}

For both types, the techno-economic models described in section three were implemented in the Python programming language, using the Pyomo package and the Gurobi solver. For the portfolio optimization, multi‑period linear programs (LPs with SOS2) were used, for which the objective was to minimize the total costs, constrained by the availability of each NET and the requirement of fulfilling the respective CDR target.\vspace{2mm}

The BECCS model is used as the representative for both BiCRS approaches. This simplification is valid because both BiCRS approaches are constrained by, and therefore compete for, the same biomass feedstock base, and the marginal abatement cost curves of both technologies exhibit a similar cost and availability range. As a result, explicitly including both models would primarily affect the allocation within the BiCRS category, but not materially impact the overall portfolio results. This modeling choice reflects a complexity-conscious implementation of the BiCRS pathways, but does not imply that BECCS and biochar are identical on a technological level. The cost of ERW is modeled using a piecewise linear function derived from the MACC, as created above, in order to allow the model to choose any operating level and compute the resulting total costs without introducing nonlinearities. Since the capital expenditures of ERW make up less than 2\% of its LCOC, using the MACC as a fixed, exogenous input, instead of separately modeling the capital investments, is a sufficiently realistic approximation. The same appraoch was used for BECCS in the static models. For the dynamic BECCS model, the fixed and variable costs and the capital investments were modeled separately, meaning that the optimizer chooses not only the operating level for BECCS, but also the timeline for capacity construction. Piecewise functions were also applied for the rock use of ERW and the learning curve of DAC. In Pyomo, they were implemented via SOS2 piecewise constraints, which approximate nonlinear cumulative cost curves. The CapEx for DAC are defined by a bilinear function of the amount built in a given year and the learning-curve based cost per unit. A McCormick envelope \parencite{mccormick_computability_1976} was used as a linear approximation for this function by introducing an auxiliary variable for CapEx and four linear constraints with variable bounds. The A/R model is linear and does not require the use of piecewise approximation.\vspace{2mm}

The following restrictions were considered in the optimization models: A/R is restricted by the growth of trees, the total available land area, and a limitation on the annual reforested area relative to all available land. In addition, an annual reversal rate is implemented to simulate fires and pests. The BECCS model is limited by the available amount of waste biomass, an initial availability of electric power capacity, and an annual maximum capacity increase of, while the technology itself is assumed to be available at a constant construction cost. ERW is restricted by the agricultural land area available for spreading, as well as a limitation of annual mining capacity relative to the global coal mining industry, which increases linearly over the operating years. DAC is only limited by an assumed initial capacity and a maximum annual capacity increase of relative to the previous year's capacity.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Static Portfolios}

From the static portfolios shown in figures 4.1 and 4.2, a number of conclusions can be drawn. First of all, the target formulation has a significant impact on the results. With the annual constraint, the removals must be delivered immediately, which forces the use of more costly BECCS removals to cover the gap left by cheaper but slower A/R and ERW. Because A/R has annual planting limits and a growth lag, it is not able to fully satisfy short-term targets on its own, especially not in the case of annual constraints. Enhanced weathering faces similar constraints regarding its expansion and use of rock material, and therefore plays only a very minor role, while BECCS is deployable much quicker.\footnote{However, it should be noted that the deployability of BECCS is partially due to the model design, which accepts its use as a "gap filler" that is required to be able to achieve the annual target from the first year.} Additionally, the annual constraint creates an overshoot effect for the 50 year time horizon: From a cost perspective, it is cheaper to plant an excessive amount of trees early on than to rely on the more expensive alternative NET instead, even if that leads to over-achieving the target during the later years. Without the annual removal constraint, the optimizer can delay removals from the first year to later times. This temporal flexibility allows it to rely nearly entirely on the cheaper A/R approach, and makes engineered CDR entirely disappear for the longer time horizons. Lastly, it is worth noting that DAC sees no application in either of the scenarios due to its high costs compared to the other NET's, making it non-competitive under the static optimization model.\vspace{2mm}

The stark difference in costs between the long and short time-frames is similarily explained not only by the increased use of cheaper A/R in the longer time-frames, but also the fact that A/R itself becomes cheaper the longer the trees are allowed to grow. This is due to the fact that A/R is largely defined by its upfront costs for forest restoration, while carbon removal occurs over the entire growth cycle of 30 to 50 years. In the longer time-frames, each tree therefore removes more carbon, making A/R more efficient and decreasing its LCOC by an order of magnitude.\vspace{2mm}

From the perspective of VCM participants, this makes A/R the most attractive approach. For example, if a company wanted to achieve the goal of "historic carbon neutrality" by removing the sum of its past emissions in a one-off project, the model suggests that simply buying and retiring carbon removal credits from afforestation is the most economical solution.\vspace{2mm}

These results illustrate the limitations of a simple cost-based approach to selecting negative-emission technologies discussed in chapter 2 of this thesis. Under these static constraints, low-cost removals dominate the solution, especially if temporal flexibility is available, while novel NET are used only to fill near-term gaps. The BECCS and EWR approaches serve as temporary "compliance technologies" and Direct Air Capture is not deployed at all. This result carries two issues common for such a static, present-cost based perspective: First, the potential cost reduction due to increased deployment, and the connected learning effects especially of DAC, is entirely disregarded. However, even if cost reductions were considered, A/R would likely remain the cheapest option. The second, and more fundamental, issue is that this type of model treats the low-cost attractiveness of A/R as if it was indefinitely available, ignoring the limited nature of A/R, which is in reality constrained by the global land area available for forest restoration. Once this limited potential is exhausted, reliance on A/R alone is no longer possible. Early investment in engineered NET may therefore be necessary not because they are currently the cost-optimal option, but to drive down future costs and to ensure long-term CDR availability once cheaper approaches hit their natural limits.

\subsection{Dynamic Portfolio}

The dynamic portfolio shows staggering numbers, both, in regard to the total amount of carbon removed until the year 2100, and in regard to the total investment and operating expenditures necessary to achieve this target. For comparison, the 855 Gt of CO$_2$ requiring removal over the next 75 years are approximately equivalent to the entire historic emission of humanity until 1990, and slightly below the sum of emissions since 1990, which amounts to about 1000 Gt \parencite{ritchie_co_2020}.\vspace{2mm}

The development of the LCOC of the various technologies also provides interesting insights. The nearly flat cost of ERW is caused largely by the modeling constraints, which limit the actual deployment of the technology to a point significantly below the hypothetical potential shown by the MACC, which was developed in section 3.3. The increasing LCOC of A/R, and to a more significant degree, of BECCS, are explained by the fact that the optimizer uses the cheapest potential first, e.g. the tropic rainforests with very fast growth and high biomass accumulation, or the cheaply available manure for BECCS. Once these options are are exhausted, it moves to add the more expensive options from less economical areas or input materials. By the mid-2040s, both, A/R and BECCS, will reach their maximum annual potential. This means that while they will keep contributing significant amounts of CDR, their expansion will be stopped by natural limitations in land and waste biomass availability. Finally, the results show that A/R removals start to decline from the mid-2060s, when all available land is used up and the maturing forests start to absorb less and less CO$_2$ from the atmosphere.\vspace{2mm}

Looking at DAC, the fact that its LCOC seems to only decreases slightly after sharp initial drop can be traced to the nature of the learning effects: The amount of DAC capacity constructed under the early, high-cost conditions makes up only a small share of the total DAC capacity. By the time the 100th Mt of capacity is deployed, the cost will already have fallen to below 1000 USD, and by the first Gt, it will be below 500 USD.\vspace{2mm}

\subsection{Interpreting the techno-economic potential and cost}
Returning to research question one, Table 4.2 provides an overview of the CDR potential and LCOC ranges of the four approaches modeled in this chapter. The models find that afforestation is the cheapest NET overall, with most of the potential being available at below 10 USD / t CO2. However, this potential is limited by the land availability and biomass growth cycle. It therefore dominates in the short-term, but eventually saturates and needs to be replaced by engineered removal options from the mid-2060s onwards.\vspace{2mm}

Enhanced weathering also shows enormous potential of nearly 10 Gt CO2 / yr at costs of no more than 70 USD / t CO2, which is significantly cheaper than the remaining options. However, it is severely limited by the logistical constraints of building what is effectively a large-scale, global mining operation similar in size to the present-day coal industry. Therefore, despite its potential, ERW only makes up about 5\% of the cumulative removals under realistic assumptions, despite being cost-competitive.\vspace{2mm}

The BiCRS approach, represented here by the BECCS model, is a critical in filling the middle period. With a potential of nearly 5 Gt CO2 / yr, it acts as a "bridge" that buys time for DAC to scale up sufficiently. Being available from about 120 dollars, it sits between A/R and ERW on the cheap end and DAC on the expensive side. Also, compared to other approaches, it is the only one with significant co-revenues, in this case from electricity generation, which significantly improve its economics.\vspace{2mm}

Finally, DAC is the most expensive of all options, but plays a critical role in the later part of the century, since it is the only option that has a technically unlimited potential for CDR, being limited virtually only by the energy available.\vspace{2mm}

Compared to the previous literature, as reviewed in section 2, the cost and CDR potential modeled generally fall within or close to the same ranges (see table 2.2), with the exception of ERW, which was found to be available at a minimum cost about 50\% below the literature estimates.

\subsection{The cost-minimizing portfolio}
Research question two represents the core contribution of this thesis: The question of how the ideal, cost-minimizing portfolio of negative emissions technologies should be designed in order to meet the climate goals of the Paris agreement. The dynamic portfolio presented in figure 4.3 and discussed above proposes one potential solution to this question. The proposed portfolio is unsurprisingly one that includes several technologies that work together: Afforestation begins immediately due to its simplicity and cheap cost, while BECCS covers the requirements that A/R cannot meet due to natural limitations. Finally, DAC comes into operation in the mid-century and takes over the bulk of the CDR in the later decades.\vspace{2mm}

The actual critical findings, however, are the development of DAC capacity compared to its operations, and the role of ERW. DAC construction begins earlier than its LCOC curve, which only starts from the point where DAC commences operations in the mid-2040s. Investing in such plants, only for them to sit idle for nearly their entire lifetime, seems counterintuitive. However, it makes sense considering that operating them is still more expensive than using the other NET as long as these can fulfill the annual CDR target. The initial DAC investment therefore occurs not to actually contribute to the CDR target of those years, but to build up capacity and to be ready when it is required, while "buying down" the average capital expenditures per tonne of capacity. Effectively, the early DAC investments act as insurance against the future unavailability of sufficient CDR options come the saturation of cheaper NET. Therefore, these results should be regarded as an encouragement to begin DAC deployment early in order to drive down its costs by the time it is required in significant amounts from the mid-2040s onwards.\vspace{2mm}

The role of enhanced weathering is probably the biggest point of debate in these results. As the MACC developed before (figure 3.9) shows, ERW has the potential to achieve about 9 Gt of CDR per year, which would alone be sufficient to cover nearly half of the annual CDR required by the year 2100. Yet, its role in the final portfolio is diminished to a mere 1 Gt annualy, based on limitations of rock availability and logistics. This result can be interpreted in two ways: Either, one could argue that enhanced weathering is not worth pursuing due to the inherent operational problems. At the same time, looking at the LCOC of below 70 USD / t, it could be argued that all available efforts should be made to makes this technology more available.\vspace{2mm}

The latter argument raises the question of the cost of this ideal portfolio. The total cost of 136 trillion USD over the next 75 years is so large that it exceeds the total annual gross domestic product of the entire world, which is expected to reach 123 trillion USD in 2026 \parencite{imf_gdp_2026}. In other words, this portfolio would require about 1.5\% of the global GDP per year to be spent on carbon removal. While this number doesn't seem that large, it should be noted that this would require every country in the world to contribute this amount to a coordinated, global effort. Considering the current state of international relations, which are increasingly dominated by opposing "spheres of influence", the success of such a coordinated efforts seems doubtful. Regardless of the way of its implementation, the investments that will be required to achieve sufficient carbon removal will probably have a huge impact on the global economy due to the diversion of investments away from economic growth and towards negative emissions technologies, which do not directly contribute to value creation.

\subsection{Impact of social and political constraints}
Looking at the high cost of the proposed portfolio and the fact that these technologies do not create economic value in themselves, the question of political and social feasibility arises. Although it can (and should) be argued that limiting climate change is of direct benefit to society and humanity as a whole, the long-term perspective and intangibility of this benefit likely put these efforts at odds with the relatively short election cycles of western democracies. But even in more authoritarian systems, the incentive for individual politicians to divert significant funding to such projects remains at least doubtful.\vspace{2mm}

Additionally, each technology (save A/R) is likely to face individual opposition. The case of enhanced weathering discussed above is the starkest example of political and social limitations: Despite huge potential and relatively low cost, the impact of this approach is expected to be very low due to the assumed resistance to the development of large-scale global mining operations, which would exceed global coal mining if the full potential of ERW was to be achieved. Besides ERW, other approaches also face political obstacles: The general acceptance of BECCS, for example, is presumably much higher than that of ERW, but carbon transport through pipelines and long-term carbon storage could face significant opposition, which is currently not considered in the optimization model. This issue naturally also concerns direct air capture. Additionally, DAC requires significant amounts of energy, which is a limited resource with intense competition from applications such as AI and electric vehicles of various types. Greatly increased energy demand from DAC could increase energy shortages and drive up prices, which may lead to opposition against using large amounts of energy for a technology that has little to no immediate, short-term benefits.\vspace{2mm}

In sum, it should be noted that the purely technical optimization vastly diverges from a socially and politically feasible one. The present model attempts to capture some of the most pressing social and political concerns in order to present a realistic picture of CDR development, but deeper analysis of social and political factors is required to ensure that the solutions proposed from a technological, long-term perspective don't get derailed by the reality of politics and public opinion.

\subsection{Implications for investors and policy makers}
Finally, the question arises as to which conclusions the two primary addressees of this study, the investors who either build or invest in negative emissions, and the policymakers who are responsible for the political framework of the global response to climate change, should draw from the results presented above.\vspace{2mm}

For investors, the primary learning is that most investments in engineered NET are strategic investments in the long-term future of carbon removals. This is especially true for DAC, which is not economically viable at the moment, but will become highly relevant in the second half of the 21st century. Considering the learning effects considered above, investing now is an important step to secure the viability of DAC in the future, and those who can make it available at scale from the 2040s onwards will likely profit from an ever-increasing demand. Still, investors must consider that this projection and the results of this thesis rely on an expanding demand for CDR, which depends on political enforcement of the targets to limit climate change set in the Paris agreement. However, most political frameworks, such as the EU ETS, still have to implement the use of CRC. Additionally, if unexpected changes in the currently volatile political environment led to a global abandonment of these targets and other goals of limiting climate change, investments in negative emissions would lose significant value and would rely solely on voluntary purchases by climate-conscious buyers.\vspace{2mm}

For policymakers, this means that the most important goal regarding negative emissions should be to implement laws and regulations that allow the use of CRC to offset residual emissions, and provide long-term security regarding climate change targets and the role negative emissions will play in the future. This, in turn, requires holistic frameworks to govern carbon markets, including, but not limited to, the quality of carbon removals. The EU Carbon Removals and Carbon Farming Regulation \parencite{european_commission_carbon_2025} is a first step in this direction, but still does not differentiate between present price considerations and future capacity requirements. Additionally, as discussed in section 2.3, politicians must also consider if and how NET should be subsidized to create sufficient incentive for CDR investments, while maintaining the ongoing carbon abatement efforts and convincing the public of the need of such public investments.

\subsection{Limitations}
The dynamic optimization model created in this thesis relies on the goal of limiting climate change agreed upon in the Paris agreement of 2015, and the carbon removal targets implied by this agreement, as set out by the UN Environment Programme Emissions Gap Report \parencite{unep_emissions_2017}. Faster decarbonization may significantly reduce the need for CDR, while slower decarbonization would increase it. A sensitivity analysis using various CDR targets would be useful to quantify these effects.\vspace{2mm}

Further, the models assume energy, especially electricity, to be available in ulimited quantities at present day prices. In a highly electrified, AI-driven future, energy scarcity could significantly change the economics of NET, especially DAC. Modeling various scenarios of energy supplies, both regarding availability and carbon intensity, would be a valuable addition. The dynamic model also assumes a learning rate of 10\% for DAC. If the realized rate is lower, DAC costs could remain prohibitively expensive, while a higher learning rate could make DAC more economical. Again, a sensitivity analysis would be valuable.\vspace{2mm}

Finally, the model also excludes technologies such as direct ocean capture, ocean alkalinization, and other experimental approaches. While this is sensible considering their early stage and the resulting difficulty of creating reliable models, these technologies could become important with further research. Also, the BiCRS model uses only waste biomass in order to avoid raising the issue of land competition created by using purpose-grown energy crops grown. It would be valuable to consider how much additional biomass could be made available through dedicated plantation, since this would reduce the reliance on DAC in the second half of the century.
