\mychapter{5}{Conclusion}
In order to achieve the goal of the Paris agreement of limiting the global temperature increase to "well below" 2 °C, and increasing amount of carbon removal will be required. The UNEP estimates the annual "emissions gap" to be around 20 Gt CO$_2$ by the year 2100. It is therefore imperative to develop a portfolio of negative emissions technologies that can be used to close this gap and reach a net-zero greenhouse gas effect. This thesis find that the cost-minimizing NET portfolio consists of a combination of approaches that are deployed in sequence based on their temporal profiles and economic, technological, and political limitations. Afforestation and BiCRS are used for the near-term targets, while DAC carries the long-term burden in the second half of this century. Enhanced weathering is theoretically promising but severely restricted by logistical and social constraints. The resulting portfolio shows a total cost of 136 trillion US\$ over 75 years for CDR totaling 855 Gt CO$_2$ at an average price of 159 US\$/t.\vspace{2mm}

The central insight from the dynamic portfolio approach is that timing matters as much as cost. A static approach, focused only on minimizing present cost would put "all eggs in one basket" by betting entirely on afforestation. But A/R saturates within the coming decades as less and less additional land is available. In comparison, the engineered NET have no natural limits, with the exception of BiCRS, which relies on available biomass inputs. The time effect is especially relevant for DAC, since its cost trajectory depends on learning effects, which in turn are defined by the cumulative deployment. Delaying invstments in DAC therefore not only push back its availability, but also keep it expensive. Ultimately, no single NET can deliver the required scale on its own: The optimal combination requires all four technologies to be used in the right amount at the right time to fully use their respective potential. For all relevant NET except A/R, the potential is limited not only by technical, but also logistical and political constraints. This is especially problematic for ERW, which requires extensive mining operations, but DAC and BiCRS are also expected to face resistance regarding CO$_2$ transportation and storage. Investors and lawmakers must therefore consider the public perception and political frameworks in place when deciding on CDR pathways.\vspace{2mm}

To create certainty for CDR investments, governance frameworks that define quality standards for CRC should be established, and existing carbon pricing and cap-and-trade systems, such as the EU ETS, should move to allow the use of high-quality carbon removals to offset corporate carbon balances. To incentivize NET investments, a variety of subsidies or even public procurement and retirement of CRC should be considered where existing voluntary carbon markets fail to direct sufficient investments to future-proof technologies.\vspace{2mm}

To conclude, this thesis has shown that in order to meet the 2 °C goal, using various types of presently available, typically cheap carbon offsets and removals of doubtful quality is not sufficient. A strategic, timed deployment of a portfolio of high-quality carbon removals is necessary to ensure that sufficient carbon removals are available to close the remaining emissions gap are available in the long term.
