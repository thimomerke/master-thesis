\mychapter{6}{Conclusion}
In order to achieve the goal of the Paris agreement of limiting the global temperature increase to "well below" 2 °C, and increasing amount of carbon removal will be required. The UNEP estimates the annual "emissions gap" to be around 20 Gt CO$_2$ by the year 2100. It is therefore imperative to develop a portfolio of negative emissions technologies that can be used to close this gap and reach a net-zero greenhouse gas effect. This thesis find that the cost-minimizing NET portfolio consists of a combination of approaches that are deployed in sequence based on their temporal profiles and economic, technological, and political limitations. Afforestation and BiCRS are used for the near-term targets, while DAC carries the long-term burden in the second half of this century. Enhanced weathering is theoretically promising but severely restricted by logistical and social constraints. The resulting portfolio shows a total cost of 136 trillion US\$ over 75 years for CDR totaling 855 Gt CO$_2$ at an average price of 159 US\$/t.\vspace{2mm}

The central insight from the dynamic portfolio approach is that timing matters as much as cost. The dynamic CDR portfolios developed in this thesis address the governance paradox discussed in chapter 1. By explicitly modeling future NET requirements and technology learning, this approach moves beyond the primarily price-driven voluntary carbon markets that currently dominate. A static approach, focused only on minimizing present cost would put "all eggs in one basket" by betting entirely on afforestation. However, A/R saturates within the coming decades as less and less additional land is available. In comparison, the engineered NET have no natural limits, with the exception of BiCRS, which relies on available biomass inputs. The time effect is especially relevant for DAC, since its cost trajectory depends on learning effects, which in turn are defined by the cumulative deployment. Delaying investments in DAC therefore not only push back its availability, but also keep it expensive. Ultimately, no single NET can deliver the required scale on its own: The optimal combination requires all four technologies to be deployed in the right amount at the right time to fully exploit their respective potential. For all relevant NET except A/R, the potential is limited not only by technical but also logistical and political constraints. This is especially problematic for ERW, which requires extensive mining operations, but DAC and BiCRS are also expected to face resistance regarding CO$_2$ transportation and storage. Policymakers can use these dynamic portfolios to design strategies and subsidy programs that incentivize investments in high-durability, scalable technologies (DAC, ERW, partially BiCRS) while managing the transition away from land-limited options like A/R. Investors can use it as a roadmap for phased CDR purchases aligned with long-term net-zero targets.\vspace{2mm}

To create certainty for CDR investments, governance frameworks that define quality standards for CRC should be established, and existing carbon pricing and cap-and-trade systems, such as the EU ETS, should move to allow the use of high-quality carbon removals to offset corporate carbon balances. To incentivize NET investments, a variety of subsidies or even public procurement and retirement of CRC should be considered where existing voluntary carbon markets fail to direct sufficient investments to future-proof technologies.\vspace{2mm}

To conclude, this thesis has shown that in order to meet the 2 °C goal, using the various types of currently available, typically cheap carbon offsets and removals of doubtful quality and permanence is not sufficient. A strategic, timed deployment of a portfolio of high-quality, permanent NET is necessary to ensure that sufficient carbon removals are available to close the remaining emissions gap in the long term while balancing cost, scalability, and societal acceptance.
