\mychapter{2}{Present state of CDR}
This section presents an overview of the present state of carbon removal, based on the recent developments of  carbon markets, literature on the global governance mechanisms of carbon removal, and the four negative emission technologies that were selected based on their maturity and prevalence, both in terms of research literature and actual deployment.

\section{Relevance of CDR and Carbon Markets}
The \textcite{unep_emissions_2017} expects that, in addition to stringent emission reductions, about 10 Gt of annual CDR will be required by 2050, rising to 20 Gt annually by 2100. \textcite{breyer_proposing_2023} estimate the overall CDR required to achieve the 1.5 °C target to be at least 500 Gt over the course of the remaining 21st century. Note that these numbers rely on the agreed-upon emissions reduction pathway of the Paris agreement. In reality, according to the Emissions Gap Report 2025 \parencite{unep_emissions_2025}, global CO$_2$ emissions keep rising. The longer these emission reduction pathways are delayed, the more they will rely on even greater amounts of CDR to close the emissions gap.\vspace{2mm}

In comparison, according to \textcite{cdrfyi_2025_2025}, only about 15 Mt of CDR were contracted, but not yet delivered, in the second quarter of 2025. Of those, more than 90\% were contracted by a single corporate buyer and are based on BECCS projects. This aligns with the findings by \textcite{roston_market_2025}, who argues that the VCMs, despite being the primary driver of early-stage CDR projects, suffer from a lack of standardized products, face liquidity issues, and, through dependence on few but large transactions, prevent efficient price discovery. It is therefore imperative to move beyond the reliance on present VCMs to understand the optimal composition of future CDR portfolios, and instead analyze the available approaches based on their (projected) availability, cost, and ultimately their ability to achieve an increase of annual CDR by at least two orders of magnitude until 2050.

\section{Defining CDR}
There is a wide range of terminology and definitions in the carbon removal industry. The most common terms include carbon dioxide removal (CDR) and synonyms such as greenhouse gas removal (GGR) or simply carbon removal.\footnote{GGR technically includes the removal of non-CO$_2$ greenhouse gases (GHGs), however, none of these can presently be removed at scale \parencite{net_zero_climate_greenhouse_nodate}.} The different approaches used to conduct CDR are typically referred to as negative emission technologies (NETs), irrespective of whether it is a natural, engineered or hybrid approach. Finally, the use of NETs to conduct CDR yields what is typically referred to as a carbon removal credit (CRC) - the claim on one tonne of CO$_2$ removed from the atmosphere. The use of such CRCs by companies to offset their carbon balance is referred to as "retiring" the CRCs.\vspace{2mm}

\textcite{smith_state_2024} define CDR by the three principles of (1) removing carbon from the atmosphere, (2) using durable storage, (3) as the result of active human intervention. These principles align with the \textcite{ipcc_global_2019} report on global warming, which defines CDR as "anthropogenic activities removing CO$_2$ from the atmosphere and durably storing it in geological, terrestrial or ocean reservoirs, or in products." This definition is crucial, since it clarifies that point capture (CCS), e.g. at fossil power plants, and avoidance projects aimed on the prevention of GHG emissions, do not qualify as CDR. It is also intuitive, considering that the prevention of emissions results in a net-zero, and not a net-negative, effect and using such credits to offset additional carbon emissions would result in double-counting \parencite{reichelstein_accounting_2024}. The portfolio design will therefore be driven by a distinction between quantity and quality of CDR, and only consider NETs that follow these three principles.\vspace{2mm}

The cost of carbon removal is typically referred to as the levelized cost of carbon captured (LCOC). The baseline LCOC is calculated from the net operating costs and capital expenditures divided by the net carbon removed. From the base LCOC, a number of further terms are derived. The LCOC$^+$ includes the leakage from the carbon removal process, e.g. emissions from energy use, which is particularily relevant for direct air capture. The LCOCR additionally includes the transport and storage costs, while the LCOCR$^+$ also considers the leakage from production, transport and storage. Finally the LCOCR$^{+*}$ deducts any co-revenues generated, for example from the energy produced by BECCS.

\section{CDR Governance}
The governance of carbon removal, and global carbon emissions in general, is still lacking, making the comparison of different carbon credit offerings difficult, and the existing carbon markets highly intransparent \parencite{roston_market_2025}. Research by \textcite{trencher_demand_2024} has shown that nearly 90\% of CRCs retired by companies between 2020 and 2023 are cheap, low-quality offsets that likely do not provide real emission reductions.\vspace{2mm}

A useful way of understanding this issue is to draw a comparison to money: Economists generally follow the philosophy of "a dollar is a dollar is a dollar" - the fungibility assumption declares that each dollar (or Euro, or any other currency) is a perfect substitute for any other dollar (or Euro, etc.) \parencite{zelizer_dollar_2017}. Fundamentally, this assumptions should also apply to CO$_2$: Each tonne of carbon dioxide emitted to the atmosphere is as good (or rather, bad) as any other tonne anywhere else in the world - the global warming effect is the same \parencite{ritchie_co_2023}.\vspace{2mm}

However, in the carbon removal market, as the vast price differences for CRCs show, the fungibility assumption obviously fails \parencite{roston_market_2025}. Otherwise, why would someone pay 500 USD for a CRC, if another is available for a mere 4 USD \parencite{reichelstein_accounting_2024} ? The root cause of these discrepancies lies in the fact that while a tonne of carbon emitted is always worth the same (negative) value, a tonne removed is decidedly not. Instead, the quality, and thus economic value, of a CRC is defined by its (1) verifiability, (2) additionality and (3) permanence. Other considerations also include accounting, monitoring, or avoidance of negative side-effects, but these can largely be summarized under the former three categories \parencite{axelsson_revised_2024}.\vspace{2mm}

The first two categories, verifiability and additionality, are both questions of quantification: How can we ensure that any given CRC is in fact backed by real carbon removed from the atmosphere, and that this carbon removed amounts to one net tonne? On the other hand, permanence is a question of storage: How can we ensure that this tonne of CO$_2$ is stored in such a manner that it will stay out of the atmosphere for at least the promised period of time? \parencite{loffler_2025_2025}\vspace{2mm}

There are a number of different frameworks that try to solve these governance problems, such as Gold Standard, Verra, or the GCC regulatory framework, among many others. However, an analysis of green-washing scandals and VCM failures by \textcite{sasaki_addressing_2025} showed that even those frameworks have not been able to consistently ensure the quality of CRCs certified through them. More recently, governments have also started to adopt their own CDR verification frameworks. The European Union's "Carbon Removals and Carbon Farming" regulation aims to establish an EU-wide taxonomy of NETs considered permanent, and standards to ensure quality, monitoring, and reporting, while, importantly, facilitating investments in novel carbon removal technologies \parencite{european_parliament_regulation_2024}.\vspace{2mm}

Carbon removal governance also includes the question of how CDR should be funded, since the traditional industrial-economic system does not provide any direct incentive to its actors to spend money on reducing or even removing carbon emissions. One option is to integrate (high-quality, verified) CRCs into existing emissions-trading systems, such as the EU ETS. This would transform the EU ETS from a cap-and-trade system with politically defined emission limits into a system in which both emissions abatement and carbon removal contribute to compliance. The resulting carbon price would then be determined by the marginal cost of the least-cost abatement or removal option, such that both marginal abatement cost and marginal removal cost equal the equilibrium carbon price \parencite{rickels_integrating_2021}. However, this unconditional use of CDR comes with a range of issues. On one hand, it could undermine emission reductions efforts if CDR is available too cheaply. Conversely, if CDR turns out to be too expensive, it would not create any incentives to actually invest in NETs, making it ineffective. The literature suggest that in both cases, public institutions could resolve this by either limiting the use of CRCs in the ETS, or serving as an intermediary that funds investments into NETs and sells the resulting CRCs below cost or simply retires them itself, effectively subsidizing NET development and deployment \parencite{edenhofer_economics_2024}.\vspace{2mm}

Finally, CDR governance must not focus on projects and technologies alone, but rather on portfolios. As will be discussed below, the various NETs each come with trade-offs and limitations that go beyond the cost and quality of the resulting CRCs. While the continued use of cheap, low-quality CRCs is a significant concern, the solution is not as simple as investing only in the most permanent NETs based on deep geological storage and abandoning all other approaches. Instead, approaching CDR from a portfolio perspective, where the sum of the various NETs deployed fulfills the requirements regarding quantity and quality of carbon removals, should inform future policy \parencite{braun_global_2022}.

\section{CDR Approaches and Maturity}
The literature broadly categorizes NETs into nature-based solutions, engineered ("novel") solutions, and hybrid apporaches. Each category presents specific trade-offs between the cost, scalability, and quality of the removals.

\subsection{Afforestation and Reforestation}
Afforestation and reforestation is the most mature CDR technology due to the extensive human knowledge about forestry, far predating the advent of the issue of climate change caused by anthropogenic CO$_2$ emissions as a result of the industrial revolution. In his treatise \textit{Sylvicultura oeconomica}, Hans Carl von Carlowitz noted more than 300 years ago that full forest regeneration can take up to a century, depending on the climate, soil conditions and type of tree, and discussed ways to increase forest growth through manual planting \parencite[pp.~188-201]{von_carlowitz_sylvicultura_1713}.\vspace{2mm}

Presently, global forests already sequester 7.6 Gt of CO$_2$ annually without human intervention \parencite{harris_global_2021}. Therefore, A/R benefits from a long history, low technological barriers, and generally shows favorable economics compared to other CDR options \parencite{fuss_negative_2018}. However, A/R is inherently vulnerable to natural disasters such as wildfires, droughts, and pests, which can lead to partial or total reversal of these removals \parencite{wong_can_2003}. This concern is elevated by the increase in such natural disasters due to climate change \parencite{liang_climate_2025}.\vspace{2mm}

Finally, the sequestration potential of A/R is dependent on the available land area and the environmental conditions in those areas, which significantly impact the amount of biomass accumulated \parencite[4.69]{eggleston_2006_2006}. These differences show the need for spatial differentiation of A/R efforts, and region-focused strategies instead of a blind, global push to maximize reforested areas \parencite{liang_climate_2025}.

\subsection{Biomass Carbon Removal and Storage}
Biomass Carbon Removal and Storage is another hybrid approach, and includes different technologies based on the use of biomass. Generally, biomass that sequestered CO$_2$ during its growth is collected and processed into a more permanent form of carbon storage, either through combustion combined with carbon capture and storage in the case of Bioenergy with Carbon Capture and Storage, or through pyrolysis (thermal decomposition without oxygen) in the case of Biochar.\vspace{2mm}

BiCRS benefits from a wide range of potential input materials, such as agricultural and forestry waste, animal manure, municipal solid waste, or purpose-grown energy crops \parencite{dipple_building_2021}, and its component technologies are in a relatively mature stage. Additionally, both approaches can deliver significant co-benefits. BECCS generates heat, which can be converted into energy \parencite{hanssen_biomass_2020}, while pyrolysis yields biochar that can be applied to agricultural soil and was found to increase soil microbial biomass, while remaining stable for centuries \parencite{biswal_use_2025}. While BECCS is more suitable for large-scale application, e.g., by refitting coal power plants with CCS and biomass co-firing \parencite{jones_delivering_2021}, Biochar can be deployed in a decentralized fashion using container-sized pyrolysis modules \parencite{horizon_europe_exploiting_2025}.\vspace{2mm}

Compared to ERW, BiCRS generally comes with fewer potential downsides and uncertainties. One risk is the fact that BECCS systems are highly sensitive to emissions along the supply chain and the efficiency of the CCS technology \parencite{jones_delivering_2021}. On the other hand, biochar application to agricultural soil creates some risk of introducing heavy metals and organic toxicants into the food chain, but only at levels not considered health risks \parencite{uba_chancen_2016}. Finally, the use of purpose-grown energy crops always creates a certain amount of biomass competition and land competition with other agricultural applications, as well as land areas available for A/R \parencite{uba_chancen_2016, zhao_trade-offs_2024}.

\subsection{Enhanced Rock Weathering}
Enhanced rock weathering involves spreading finely ground silicate-rich rock material (e.g., from basalt or other olivine-rich rock) on land in order to use and accelerate the natural weathering process of carbon mineralization. It is viewed as a hybrid solution, sitting in between natural and engineered approaches \parencite{manning_enhanced_2025}, and is presently in early-stage laboratory and field tests. Recent field trials, e.g. by the "Carbon Drawdown Initiative", have focused on the technical feasibility of spreading the ground rock and the weathering speed of a variety of different rock and soil combinations and cited mixed results and a need for further research \parencite{carbon_drawdown_initiative_how_2022, hammes_soil_2025}. \textcite{madankan_larger_2025} argue that large extraction sites and optimized logistics for processing and transport are essential to make ERW a viable approach.\vspace{2mm}

The primary advantages of ERW over A/R are that it offers highly durable storage in the form of liquid carbonates, and the abundant availability of the input rock materials across the globe \parencite{lackner_progress_1997}. However, as \textcite{gaucher_leveraging_2025} argue, while ERW can  improve soil fertility and decrease acidification, the accumulation of heavy metals contained in the rock dust, their impact on the soil biome, and their potential introduction into the human food chain require careful monitoring. Additionally, and similar to A/R, the weathering process is heavily dependent on the surrounding climate conditions \parencite{hangx_coastal_2009}.

\subsection{Direct Air Capture}
Direct Air Capture is a chemical process based on the capture of CO$_2$ directly from the air using solid sorbents (S-DAC) or liquid solvents (L-DAC). Combined with geological storage solutions, it can theoretically provide the highest permanence and easy quantification, allowing for high-quality carbon removal \parencite{stolten_neue_2022}. Additionaly, it is theoretically location-independent, since the only required inputs are energy and water. However, in reality, the availability of low-carbon, renewable energy sources may limit suitable geographies for DAC \parencite{eke_comprehensive_2025}. Using fossil energy sources is an option, but greatly reduces the efficiency of the entire process due to the additional carbon emissions, decreasing the net amount removed and increasing the net price of CDR.\vspace{2mm}

The main drawback of DAC is the massive energy input necessitated by the thermodynamic limits of seperating CO$_2$ from ambient air, which has relatively low concentrations of carbon dioxide compared to point sources \parencite{lackner_thermodynamics_2013}. Additionally, capital expenses for the construction of DAC plants are also  still high at present, with \textcite{young_cost_2022} suggesting that costs could decrease significantly with increased deployment, similar to the learning effects experienced by solar PV.

\subsection{Other Approaches}
There is a wide range of other approaches to carbon removal, such as Ocean Fertilization (OF), Direct Ocean Capture (DOC), and Biomass burial / sinking. However, these methods either suffer from a range of side effects that make verification of impacts extremely hard (such as in the case of Ocean Fertilization) or are so early in their technological readiness and adoption  that an attempt at modeling them economically would constitute significant guesswork \parencite{smith_state_2024}.

\section{Historic Development}
While the various approaches discussed above have in theory existed for many years, their implementation dedicated to carbon removal has only started in the late 2010s. The data aggragation platform CDR.fyi has tracked the sales and deliveries of CRCs since 2019. According to their data, by February 2026, a total of 44 Mt of CDR were sold at a total cost of 11B USD. Of these CRCs sold, only 1.3 Mt (2.95\%) were already delivered. This stark discrepancy between CRCs contracted and delivered shows that while the annual growth of the market seems impressive, it still remains to be seen how fast these promised removal can actually be delivered \parencite{cdrfyi_cdr_2026}.\vspace{2mm}

The landscape of CRC suppliers and purchasers (Fig. 2.2) presents a similar picture. While the early years from 2019 to 2021 saw a large number of buyers and sellers contracting very small amounts of CDR, the market was "kickstarted" in 2022 by a single, large BECCS project contracted between Britain-based Drax and and the CDR investor Respira. Since then, the supply side has seen some diversification, with the three largest suppliers making up about half of the market in 2025. In contrast, the demand side continues to be firmly dominated by very few, very large purchasers, the market share of which has even increased in recent years. This development suggests that the CDR market is a strong buyers market that is heavily dependent on and driven by the interests and behaviors of a few individual actors, and far away from the goal of a liquid market that dynamically finds the ideal capital allocation to fulfill the targets of the Paris agreement in the most cost-efficient way. Research suggests that this is due to a lack of integration of CDR technologies and markets into existing environmental policy, as discussed in 2.3, and warns of the risk of creating a \textit{valley of death} where early-stage projects fail before being able to scale to a sustainable size \parencite{schenuit_scaling_2025}. Consequently, governments should incentivize investments in a diverse portfolio of NET's.

\section{Comparative Economics and Scalability}
There is a wide range of cost and capacity estimates from research and various syntheses on carbon removal, its economics and potential, such as the landmark study by \textcite{fuss_negative_2018} or the \textit{CDR Primer} by \textcite{dipple_building_2021}. This section gives an overview of the cost and capacity ranges of the four relevant NETs as discussed by the previous literature.
\subsection{Costs and Cost Trajectories}
The literature indicates that DAC is currently the most expensive NET, driven by its early stage of technological readiness, limited deployment, and the associated high capital expenditures per unit of capacity, as well as the energy-intensive nature of the approach. However, while the energy requirements have a hard floor imposed by thermodynamics \parencite{lackner_thermodynamics_2013}, the capital expenditures are assumed to drop significantly in the future due to learning effects following the increased cumulative deployment of DAC \parencite{sievert_considering_2024, young_cost_2022, dipple_building_2021}. Overall, most older literature estimates ranged between 600 and 1000 USD tCO$_2^{-1}$ for first-of-a-kind with the potential to decrease to between 100 and 300 USD tCO$_2^{-1}$ around 2050, depending on the assumed learning rates, sources of power, and potential subsidies \parencite{fuss_negative_2018}. A more recent study by \textcite{sievert_considering_2024} analyzed DAC systems and assigned learning rates at a component level, and found a less optimistic estimate of 341 USD tCO$_2^{-1}$ for L-DAC and 374 USD tCO$_2^{-1}$ for S-DAC systems at 1 Gt yr$^{-1}$ cumulative capacity. This estimate also matches currently ongoing projects, such as the \textit{Cypress} S-DAC plant under construction in Lousianna (USA) by Climeworks, which aims to achieve net costs between 400 and 600 USD tCO$_2^{-1}$ at a scale of 1.3 MtCO$_2$ yr$^{-1}$ \parencite{climeworks_climeworks_2024}.\vspace{2mm}

Significantly below the costs for DAC, the price of one tonne of CO$_2$ removed using BiCRS technologies is generally assumed to be between 50 and 250 USD. For BECCS, \textcite{fuss_negative_2018} estimated a range of 100 to 200 USD tCO$_2^{-1}$, while the range for Biochar was suggested to be between 90 and 120 USD tCO$_2^{-1}$. \textcite{fajardy_economics_2021} conclude a higher cost of 240 USD tCO$_2^{-1}$ for BECCS, arguing that the cost greatly depends on the cost of the input biomass and the revenue achieved from the electric energy produced. Similar considerations apply to Biochar, which produces not energy but biochar as a valuable output product.\vspace{2mm}

The cost of ERW is more difficult to estimate due to the relative immaturity, even compared to DAC. The reviews by \textcite{fuss_negative_2018} and \textcite{beerling_farming_2018} consequently determined a wide range of cost estimates between 50 and 578 USD tCO$_2^{-1}$, while \textcite{breunig_life_2024} more recently concluded that a cost below 100 USD is realistic. It is generally assumed that the majority of the total costs would come from the extraction, processing and transportation of the rock, while the weathering process itself causes no additional cost. However, measurement, reporting and verification (MRV) of actual carbon removed is much more difficult than for other approaches and requires soil sampling and laboratory analysis, which creates some difficulty in quantifying additional costs \parencite{reershemius_initial_2023, zani_method_2024}.\vspace{2mm}

In contrast, Afforestation and Reforestation is a very mature approach. The costs are generally based on the cost of planting or regenerating a certain area of forest and the climate conditions that define the speed of biomass accumulation. At an estimated regeneration cost of 1170 to 5200 USD per hectare (ha) of forest, and an assumed CO$_2$ accumulation of 250 t ha$^{-1}$ of forest, the cost per tonne of CO$_2$ removed would range from 4.68 to 20.80 USD \parencite{michel_estimating_2026}. Another study by \textcite{bodin_standard_2022} additionally included perpetual, annual maintenance costs, of 2 to 213 USD ha$^{-1}$. Using their estimated establishment cost of between 12 and 3880 USD and a discount rate of 7\%, this sums up to a cost per tonne of CO$_2$ removed of between 0.16 and 27.69 USD. Both studies are in line with earlier research, which found significant potential at costs between 5 and 50 USD \parencite{fuss_negative_2018, busch_cost-effectiveness_2024}.

\subsubsection{Learning Rates}
In the context of carbon removal, both researchers and practitioners often refer to "learning effects" or the idea of "buying down" the costs. This concept refers to the idea of experience-based cost reductions, where the cost declines with the increase of the cumulative manufactured or deployed quantity. It was originally observed by \textcite{wright_factors_1936} in the construction of airframes, and has more recently been used to explain the significant drop in costs of solar PV since the early 2000s. The learning rate is calculated as the reduction in unit costs with each doubling of cumulative output, and in the example of solar PV amounts to 25.7\% \parencite{philipps_photovoltaics_2025}. The most important expected learning effects are those of DAC capital expenditures per unit, which the literature expects to be between 10 and 15\%, meaning that a doubling of cumulative capacity could reduce investment costs per unit by 10 to 15\% \parencite{al-juaied_prospects_2023}. Some research suggests other possible learning effects related to CDR, such as for the MRV costs of ERW \parencite{reershemius_initial_2023}, while BiCRS-based approaches are considered rather mature and are expected to show only insignificant future learning effects \parencite{iata_carbon_2025}. Overall, all of the expected learning rates remain somewhat elusive due to the limited real-world experience and cost data regarding all NETs, and relying on high learning effects to achieve affordable CDR at sufficient scale may create the risk of missing the required targets.

\subsection{Scalability and Resource Constraints}
In regards to their scalability, all approaches are technically capable of conducting CDR at a Gigaton scale, but a range of unique constraints applies to each of them, significantly limiting the scale-up of some of them.

\subsubsection{Afforestation and Reforestation}
From a technical perspective, A/R is only limited by the land area available for forest cultivation. In this context, afforestation refers to forests regenerated in areas that were deforested in the last 50 years, while reforestation occurs in areas that were deforested earlier \parencite{unfccc_procedures_2006}\footnote{In this thesis, the terms A/R, afforestation and reforestation are used interchangeably.}. Consequently, the CDR potential of A/R is dependent on the amount of CO$_2$ that can be sequestered by the trees grown on this land, which in turn depends on the environmental and soil conditions, impacting the length of the annual growth season and the total biomass accumulation. While biomass growth rates and total biomass accumulation per hectare are high in the tropics and sub-tropics, with up to 300 tonnes of dry mass per hectare, afforestation in temperate regions is significantly less effective, with about half the dry mass accumulated per hectare. The results in boreal regions are almost an order of magnitude lower than those in the tropics \parencite[4.69]{eggleston_2006_2006}. Additionally, dense forests typically have lower surface albedo rates, meaning that they reflect a lower fraction of sunlight, compared to open woodlands, and store a larger amount of heat \parencite{bernier_boreal_2011}. This could offset up to 70\% of the carbon uptake from A/R in global drylands, showing that not all seemingly "empty space" on earth is suitable for forestry-based CDR \parencite{rohatyn_limited_2022}. \vspace{2mm}

The UNEP Emissions Gap Report of 2025 noted that 6.7 Mha of primary forests and 30 Mha of overall tree cover were lost in 2024 alone \parencite{unep_emissions_2025}. Since 2000, a total of 520 Mha of forests were destroyed, providing an upper bound estimate for the available land area for A/R \parencite{world_resource_institute_forest_2025, sims_global_2025}. The realistically available area is probably significantly lower due to conversion into farmland and other agricultural uses that prevent reforestation. According to the CMIP6 models of the IPCC, a maximum of 363 Mha of additional tree cover are possible by the year 2100, with A/R removing a maximum of 6.38 GtCO$_2$ per year, at a mean of 2.98 GtCO$_2$ per year \parencite{egerer_forestation_2025}. Previous reports by \textcite{fuss_negative_2018} and \textcite{griscom_natural_2017} estimated an upper bound of 10 GtCO$_2$ per year. Note that these estimates assume sufficient land availability. As forests are regenerated, these carbon sinks become saturated and the annual uptake decreases. The overall additional potential for carbon storage through A/R is estimated to be around 220 GtCO$_2$ \parencite{walker_global_2022}.

\subsubsection{Biomass Carbon Removal and Storage}
Carbon removal approaches based on biomass use are limited by the amount of biomass available for processing and general planetary boundaries regarding land, nutrient and water availability, similar to A/R. The primary concern in this regard is which sources of biomass are considered for use with BiCRS. Feedstocks are various and can consist of agricultural waste, waste from forestry, animal manure and municipal solid waste, or purpose-grown biomass, so called "energy crops". The potential of BiCRS has to be seen in relation to A/R: If energy crops are planted specifically for BiCRS use, this creates competition for land that might otherwise be used for A/R, as well as competition with other non-energy crops \parencite{zhao_trade-offs_2024}.\vspace{2mm}

The global potential of BECCS is estimated to be between 0.5 and 5 GtCO$_2$ per year by 2050 \parencite{fuss_negative_2018}. If only waste biomass is used, \textcite{feng_global_2024} expect a CDR potential of only about 1.5 GtCO$_2$ per year, while it also produces nearly 11 PWh of electric energy, equal to about 10\% of global energy consumption. However, this amount does not consider potential growth of available biomass, e.g. from increased forestry due to A/R or larger amounts of municipal waste produced by a growing population. If the limitation of using only waste biomass is lifted, the integrated assessment model developed by \textcite{chiquier_integrated_2025} finds a potential of as much as 12 Gt CO$_2$ per year, while using some 150 Mha of additional land for dedicated energy crops.\vspace{2mm}

For Biochar, a comprehensive literature review by \textcite{biswal_use_2025} found a CDR potential of up to 1.8 GtCO$_2$ yr$^{-1}$ under ideal conditions, which they found to be soils with high porosity and pH levels. A study by \textcite{masek_potassium_2019} found that the addition of potassium ("potassium doping") during the pyrolysis process can increase the biochar yield and retention of carbon from the input biomass, resulting in an increased global CDR potential of up to 2.6 GtCO$_2$ yr$^{-1}$. It notes that certain types of ashes could be used as a potassium source, but does not discuss the availability and feasibility of this process at scale. These number are generally limited to the use of sustainable feedstocks. If the planting of large amount of dedicated energy crops is included, the study by \textcite{chiquier_integrated_2025} finds a removal potential as high as 12 GtCO$_2$ per year, similar to that of BECCS.

\subsubsection{Enhanced Rock Weathering}
The potential and limitations of Enhanced Rock Weathering are defined by the hypothetically available area and rock on one hand, and the practical feasibility of the process on the other hand. From a strictly technical perspective, the various types of alkaline rocks that can be used for ERW are abundantly available in most regions of the world \parencite{hangx_coastal_2009, strefler_potential_2018}. To spread the finely-ground rock dust, agricultural lands are available on the scale of billions of hectares, making up 38\% of the global land surface \parencite{almaraz_methods_2022}. Based on these considerations, the theoretical potential of ERW is estimated to be in the range of multiple gigatons per year, with a lower end of 2 GtCO$_2$ yr$^{-1}$ and an upper limit of as high as 95 GtCO$_2$ yr$^{-1}$ \parencite{almaraz_methods_2022, goll_potential_2021, strefler_potential_2018, fuss_negative_2018}. A significant factor in these estimates is the alkalinity, and the resulting CDR yield, of the various types of rock sources considered for use.\vspace{2mm}

However, these studies typically do not consider the implications of the large-scale application of ERW. Only the study by \textcite{hangx_coastal_2009}, which is focused on the coastal spreading of rock dust in the Netherlands, considered the impact on the environment and noted that to achieve CDR equivalent to 30\% of global CO$_2$ emissions through ERW, "1.9 million truckloads, or 3800 shiploads, on a weekly basis" would be required, exceeding the "around 2000 vessels" available. These figures illustrate the significant logistics problem associated with ERW. In order to use the full global potential of ERW, mining and logistics operations on the scale of at least 5 Gt per year would be required \parencite{hangx_coastal_2009}. For comparison, this scale matches the global coal mining industry, which produces about 8 Gt coal per year \parencite{iea_global_2022}.

\subsubsection{Direct Air Capture}
Compared to the other NETs discussed above, Direct Air Capture faces fewer direct constrains by land or logistics. For both types of DAC, liquid and solid, the primary requirement is energy, either in the form of electricity to run the systems or heat used to remove the concentrated CO$_2$ for transport and storage (referred to as "regeneration"). Additionally, liquid DAC uses significant amounts of water. The literature estimates that in order to reach 1 Gt of CDR per year through DAC, up to 50 Gt of water and 6 to 10 Exajoules (EJ) of energy are required \parencite{iea_direct_2022, ozkan_atmospheric_2024}. This is approximately equivalent to the entire primary energy consumption of Germany in 2025 \parencite{bdew_energieversorgung_2025}. Of these 6 to 10 EJ, about 20 to 40\% are electric energy, used for the operation of air fans, while the rest is thermal energy required for the regeneration of the solvent or sorbent \parencite{ozkan_atmospheric_2024, nas_negative_2018}. The deployment of 10 GtCO$_2$ yr$^{-1}$ DAC would consume up to 40\% of the global electricity generation \parencite{herzog_getting_2024}. In comparison, the mass deployment of artificial intelligence has increased the global energy demand from data centers to about 415 TWh of electric energy, or about 1.5\% of the global generation \parencite{iea_energy_2025}. In addition, the use of fossil fuels, especially the use of natural gas to provide thermal energy, reduces the net efficiency of DAC units \parencite{eke_comprehensive_2025}. Therefore, large-scale renewable energy production is required to enable efficient and cheap DAC. Depending on the type of energy used, this also indirectly creates the land competition DAC is hoped to avoid: The IEA estimates that in order to power a 1 GtCO$_2$ yr$^{-1}$ DAC plant with solar PV, an area of 23.000 km$^2$ would be required, "equivalent to the size of Sardinia" \parencite{iea_direct_2022}. The use of renewable energies would also restrict the otherwise flexible location choice of DAC in order to avoid the capital-intensive construction of long-distance power lines, and create a trade-off with the logistics and pipeline infrastructure required to permanently store the concentrated CO$_2$. \vspace{2mm}

Considering these factors, the research on DAC generally assumes a potential of 0.5 to 5 GtCO$_2$ per year towards the mid-century, depending mostly on energy and geological storage availability \parencite{fuss_negative_2018}. There is a wide range of proposals for DAC projects, such as the re-purposing of existing industrial infrastructure, including pipelines, which could then be used to deliver CO$_2$ to storage sites \parencite{breitschopf_direct_2023}, or the fitting of S-DAC modules to various structures in urban areas \parencite{lawler_what_2022}. However, recent literature also highlights the great uncertainty of DAC deployment, with commentary from MIT researchers noting that "[p]roperly accounting for the massive amount of capital, land, and costs involved means the feasibility of deploying DAC at the gigatonne scale is highly uncertain" \parencite{herzog_getting_2024}.

\section{Social and Political Acceptance}
Social acceptance is rarely discussed in CDR literature, which typically focuses on technological and economic feasibility of different NETs. According to \textcite{wustenhagen_social_2007}, the social acceptance of renewable energy innovations is defined by the three factors of \textit{Socio-political acceptance}, \textit{Community acceptance}, and \textit{Market acceptance} (see Fig. 2.1). Socio-political acceptance refers to acceptance in the general societal context, while community acceptance is a more localized concern regarding specific projects. Market acceptance is concerned with the acceptance of new products and innovations by the various actors in the market, most importantly the investors and consumers.\vspace{2mm}

A study by \textcite{karytsas_transnational_2023} found that that social acceptance of CCS (which is not a CDR technology in itself, but a part of BECCS), DAC and CO$_2$ pipelines hinges on the perceived safety risks of large scale carbon transport and storage and the trust (or a lack thereof) in their operators. The study also finds that the "Not In My Back Yard" (NIMBY) phenomenon, in the form of resistance to specific projects, is particularly strong for this type of technology, which indicates challenges in community acceptance. A study by \textcite{bellamy_perceptions_2019}, focused specifically on the perception of BECCS, found that despite low public awareness about the technology, the initial support is strong, but major concerns include land use conflicts, geographic footprint and the safety of CO$_2$ storage. The same study concluded that the public generally supports globally coordinated persuasive and even coercive policies and fixed payments to incentivize BECCS use, but not the use of producer price guaranties \parencite{bellamy_perceptions_2019}. Another study by \textcite{fridahl_bioenergy_2018} found that the UNFCCC delegates, which include various global governmental and non-governmental actors, consider social acceptance a barrier for BECCS implementation, but put more emphasis on the lack of policy incentives and prioritization. In contrast, they describe technological readiness, or a lack thereof, as unlikely to become an issue. In sum, the literature suggests that the social acceptance of BECCS research, development and deployment, including incentive and certification schemes, is high, but significant concerns exist about the sustainability and cost for the public.\vspace{2mm}

The social barriers of technologies like ERW are less well understood, but they likely involve concerns over large-scale mining activities and dust pollution from rock crushing and spreading, at both, the socio-political and community level \parencite{manning_enhanced_2025, madankan_larger_2025}. As noted above, the amount of rock extraction that is required to conduct ERW on a gigaton scale is similar to the size of the entire global coal mining industry, and likely poses the main limiting factor for the deployment of ERW \parencite{hangx_coastal_2009}. In addition to the logistical challenges, the political and bureaucratic hurdles of such mining activities are likely significant, and resistance should be expected from environmental groups and local communities directly impacted by the proposed mining operations. Other concerns also include potential dust pollution from the finely ground rock materials and the introduction of heavy metals into the food chain \parencite{yao_life_2025, schiedung_uncertainties_2026}. Therefore, the realistic potential is probably at least an order of magnitude lower than the technical potential discussed previously.\vspace{2mm}

In contrast, the social acceptance of solutions like A/R is much higher, at least as long as it does not compete with agricultural land used for food production \parencite{schirmer_assessing_2014}. This is potentially owed to the intuitive approach of "just planting trees", but also suggests some sort of societal "naturalness bias". It may therefore be wise to consider not only the objective net effect of any given NET, but also its subjective perception in the general population, local communities and the market. A technology that is perceived as questionable or even dirty may face significant resistance on the societal and local level, even if it is objectively beneficial for the global environment at large.
