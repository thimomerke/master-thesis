\section{Biomass Carbon Removal and Storage}
BiCRS is a category of technologies which all use biomass as a feedstock to create negative emissions. The two most common technologies, Bioenergy with Carbon Capture and Storage and Biochar, are considered in this thesis.

\subsection{Biomass Availability}
Data about the global availability of waste biomass was collected from the FAOSTAT datasets on crops, livestock products and forestry \parencite{fao_faostat_2025, fao_faostat_2026}. The potential  of purpose-grown biomass was not considered in this model to avoid the issue of land competition, positioning the results on the conservative end of the range of potential estimates.\vspace{2mm}

For biomass from crop production, country-level FAO data for the production of crops was multiplied with the residue biomass generation factors from \textcite{lefebvre_biomass_2023}. The results covered 92.80\% of global crop production, while for the remaining 7.80\%, no residue factors were available. The results were subsequently multiplied with a collection factor of 30\% to account for the need to leave most of the residues in the field to avoid soil erosion and nutrient leech \parencite{battaglia_broad_2021}.\vspace{2mm}

For animal manure, a similar method was used. The country-level amount of livestock was collected from the FAOSTAT database, and multiplied with daily manure production and dry-mass collection factors from \textcite{lefebvre_biomass_2023}. These covered cattle, chicken, horses, sheep, goats, swine / pigs, making up 90.14\% of all global livestock.\vspace{2mm}

For forestry residues, the data from the FAO dataset on forestry was seperated into fuel wood and industrial wood (sawnwood). Based on an analysis by \textcite{koopmans_agricultural_1997}, it was assumed that logging residues would make up 1/3 of the raw wood cut down, and that 50\% of these residues could be harvested. For industrial woods, it was further assumed that another 50\% of the remaining 2/3 would turn into processing residues, all of which could be collected.

\subsection{Biomass Cost}
The roadside cost (i.e., the cost to deliver the biomass residues to the roadside for pickup) was calculated based on biomass type and country and adjusted for each country using purchasing power parity (PPP) data from the \textcite{world_bank_icp_2021}. For crops, data from \textcite{bain_world_2007} was used to calculate a global, inflation-adjusted average cost. For animal manure, a US-based handling cost of 5 USD per tonne was assumed, since feces is generally a waste product that holds little value, has limited use for other applications \parencite{eren_experimental_2019} except fertilization and must be disposed of. Similarily, for forest residues, a US-based price of 45 USD per tonne \parencite{skog_forest_2011} was adjusted for inflation and the country-level PPP factors. This resulted in the global marginal availability cost curve for waste biomass shown below. Note that this is a marginal availability cost curve for biomass, not a marginal removal cost curve.

\subsection{Bioenergy with Carbon Capture and Storage}

Bioenergy with Carbon Capture and Storage uses thermal power plants that generate heat from the burning of biomass, which is then used to drive steam turbines, similar to traditional coal-fired power plants. The carbon released from the biomass is then captured from the exhaust stream using CCS units and stored in geological storage sites. The BECCS model is based on an exemplary 250 MW biomass fired power plant. Assuming a capacity factor of 85\%, an annual energy output of about 1.4 GWh is generated. A 30\% baseline conversion efficiency for biomass combustion power plants was used to calculate the required amount of biomass, and a 9\% penalty was applied due to the use of CCS \parencite{cuellar_path_2015, fajardy_economics_2021}.\vspace{2mm}

For the calculation of the amount of carbon captured, the carbon content of each type of biomass was multiplied with an assumed capture rate of 90\%, and applied to the required input biomass amount. Finally, the co-revenues were calculated based on the sale of the generated net output energy at the applicable market rate. This method yielded the hypothetical amount of biomass required, carbon captured, and revenues generated per year.\vspace{2mm}

The cost of the BECCS plant is based on capital expenditures and the annual fixed costs for operation and maintenance, primarily labor and material costs, as well as some variable costs such as non-fuel consumables and waste disposal. Similarly to the biomass costs, these too were adjusted for each country using the applicable PPP factor. To calculate the LCOC, all costs were annualized and divided by the amount of carbon removed. For the final LCOCR$^{+*}$, the transport and storage costs, amounting to 24.11 USD tCO$_2^{-1}$ based on the estimate by \textcite{smith_cost_2021} for a 500 mile transport distance and medium-scale storage project, were added, the co-revenue of electricity sales was deducted, and a leakage factor of 24\% for transport and storage ($\alpha_1$) was applied.\vspace{2mm}

\subsection{Biochar}
Biochar-based carbon removal is modeled as a pyrolysis process. Pyrolysis is the thermal decomposition of biomass without oxygen, resulting in various carbon-rich products such as biochar, bio-oil and syngas, depending on the process temperature. In this model, low-temperature pyrolysis, which yields the largest fraction of solid biochar, was used. This biochar typically has a carbon content of 60 - 80\% and remains stable for centuries when applied to soil while increasing plant productivity as a co-benefit \parencite{bergero_biochar_2024}.\vspace{2mm}

To calculate the amount of carbon removed, an  exemplary pyrolysis plant was used with a capacity of 1 tonne of biomass per hour and an assumed capacity factor of 90\% (see Table 3.2). The amount of output products resulting from the pyrolysis process varies depending on the type of input biomass and its carbon content, but it was assumed that biochar has a carbon content of 80\%, while this value is 60\% for bio-oil and 25\% for syngas. The biochar and bio-oil are applied to soil or stored, and deliver the carbon removal, while the syngas is burned to produce the energy required to maintain the pyrolysis process. It was further assumed that 1400 MJ of heat would be required to maintain the pyrolysis, while the remainder could be sold for external uses, with the proceeds considered as co-revenues.\vspace{2mm}

The cost of pyrolysis, similar to BECCS, was calculated from the capital expenditures for the plant, with a lifetime of 20 years, as well as the variable costs for the input biomass and the fixed costs for maintenance and labor, less the co-revenues from the sale of excess heat. Finally, the leakage factor ($\alpha_1$) was calculated from the outputs produced depending on the input biomass, and the co-revenues from the energy sale deducted in order to calculate the LCOCR$^{+*}$. Leakage is mainly caused by biochar decay, of which only 80\% is considered permanently stored.

By feeding the above biomass availability cost curve into the BECCS and Biochar models, global marginal removal cost curves are constructed (see Figure 3.7). They show that for BECCS, using animal manure is generally the cheapest option, owed to the low assumed input costs. The overall LCOCR$^{+*}$ of BECCS ranges from 131 USD (animal manure in low-cost countries) to 333 USD (crop residues in high-cost countries). Interestingly, the picture for Biochar is very different: Forest residue-based Biochar offers the cheapest CDR at an LCOCR$^{+*}$ below 110 USD, while using animal manure is more expensive. The overall LCOCR$^{+*}$ range of Biochar is between 100 and 366 USD. Detailed MACCs, including the different cost components, are available in Appendix A.1.\vspace{2mm}

The curious difference between the two approaches is at least partially explained by modeling choices: For BECCS, the model assumes a fixed electric output capacity, and lower energy densities of inputs are simply compensated by increasing the consumption rate without impacting the power output. Therefore, cheap animal manure, which has a lower heating value and carbon content, is consumed in larger quantities, allowing for relatively cheap carbon removal. In contrast, the biochar model uses an hourly biomass processing rate, which means that the hourly biomass throughput, and not the output product, constrains the model. As a result, using feedstocks with low carbon density reduces the hourly production per unit of capacity, and therefore also the amount of CO$_2$ removed. This makes the use of high-carbon content inputs such as forest residues more attractive, while penalizing low-carbon content inputs such as rice husks.\newpage
